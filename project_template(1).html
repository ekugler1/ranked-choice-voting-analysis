Analyzing the Impact of Race and Gender in Ranked Choice Voting¶

Introduction¶

My companies goal is to increase the popularity for ranked choice voting (RCV) in the U.S. Although it has many benefits, including determining which candidate has the strongest support and reducing wasted votes, not everyone supports it. As my company sees the benefits we are putting our efforts in the form of education and advertisment campaigns to help spread the benefits of RCV. However, we want to ensure that our resources and efforts are not wasted on people that will not budge or already support it.

Therefore, we will analyze survey data in order to determine who already supports it and who might not support it. That way we can determine our best course of action and target audience. With so many variables that impact support or lack therefore, like gender, race, socioeconomic status etc. we have decided to look at two variables: gender and race. We will be using prediction analysis to determine how a person's race and gender will impact their opinions on RCV because we can tailor messages to specific racial groups, addressing their unique concerns or misunderstandings about RCV. As a company we felt this could address how different racial groups and genders have unique historical experiences with the voting system, affecting their trust. Additionally, we chose these as our key variables as past studies show racial and gender disparities. Our goal is to be a part of the solution, not the problem which is we we will use our resources wisely and insightfully.

Data Description¶

Our survey will be looking at the Pulse of a nation dataset. 800 people participated in this survey. This dataset includes variables like education, trump support, political leaning and whether they supported (yes, no or DK/REF) RCV. We will focus on gender and race. Gender in this dataset is categorized as female, male, other or DK/REF. The race variable consists of Asian, Latino, Black, White, Other and DK/REF. Inference: Hypothesis Test or Confidence Interval¶

Next our company wants to determine if there is a significant difference in the proportion of people who support ranked choice voting based on another categorical variable (such as gender or race). That is why we will use a hypothesis test. Specifically, we want to determine if there is a significant difference in the proportions of support for ranked choice voting between males vs. females for gender and whites vs. blacks for race. Therefore, we will calculate the observed statistic (difference in proportions) and use bootstrap resampling to ultimately calculate the p-value. The p-value will help us determine whether the observed difference is significant or if it is likely due to chance, giving us a more accurate understanding about where we should implement our resources and education.

def statistic(data):

    group1_data = data.where('Gender', 'Female')
    prop_female = group1_data.where('Ranked Choice', 'Yes').num_rows / group1_data.num_rows
    # males
    group2_data = data.where('Gender', 'Male')
    prop_male = group2_data.where('Ranked Choice', 'Yes').num_rows / group2_data.num_rows
    # Calculate the observed difference in proportions
    observed = prop_female - prop_male
    
    return observed

def simulation_and_statistic():
    '''
    Simulates shuffling the Gender column and calculating the difference in proportions
    of support for ranked choice voting between males and females.
    
    Returns the observed difference in proportions.
    '''
    
    shuffled_gender = data.select('Gender').sample(with_replacement=False).column('Gender')

    
    simulated_data = data.with_column('Gender', shuffled_gender)
    
    

    # Calculate the proportions for each group (males and females)
    prop_male_simulated = simulated_data.where('Gender', 'Male').where('Ranked Choice', 'Yes').num_rows / simulated_data.where('Gender', 'Male').num_rows
    prop_female_simulated = simulated_data.where('Gender', 'Female').where('Ranked Choice', 'Yes').num_rows / simulated_data.where('Gender', 'Female').num_rows

    # Calculate the difference in proportions (observed statistic)
    observed_diff_sim = prop_female_simulated - prop_male_simulated

    return observed_diff_sim

Prediction¶

Based on our data and model, the positive case is defined by the people who are marked as supporting ranked choice voting. Therefore, if there is a 1 it means yes they do support and a zero means no they do not. The goal of our model is to predict whether each individual falls into the 1 category based on their race and gender. To do this we start by removing any categories we don't want and ensuring that our categorical variables are number (1 or 0). We then will split our data into test and train and then be able to fit the models using tree something.

The model-building process starts by looking at a threshold of .5 with tree one at a depth of 1, tree 2 at a depth of 2 and tree three at a depth of 3. Based on the confusion matrix results of that we move on to trying more thresholds to ensure we find the best model. We use the accuracy_score, precision_score, and recall_score along with our original results to measure which combination ensures the best model. Our company went through this entire process and determined that the best model was the threshold of .1 and a depth of 1. This model has a precision score of 0.45625 and a recall score of 1.
